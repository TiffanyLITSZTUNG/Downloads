The Rhythm of the Year

The passenger story of 2023 and 2024 was a symphony with its diminuendos and crescendos. During the second quarter of 2023, when the cold of winter dissipated and the heat of summer began enveloping the nation, riders set a record high of 853 thousand. This increase would be attributed largely to the popularity of vacations over the summer, with families flooding destination resort areas within the United States. Coastal cities like Miami, with its stunning beaches and vibrant nightlife, saw a massive influx of visitors looking for sun, sand, and relaxation. National parks like Yellowstone, on the contrary, hosted nature enthusiasts who were interested in viewing its geothermal attractions and diverse wildlife.

However, as the year rolled on, the passenger numbers began to ebb and flow in response to a multitude of factors. In the third quarter, despite the continued pleasant weather in many regions, the numbers started to decline. This can be partly attributed to the conclusion of the peak of the holiday season and the return to classes at schools and universities. The majority of families had already gone on their summer holidays, and business travel, while still vibrant, did not entirely compensate for the drop in leisure travel. Additionally, economic circumstances and uncertainty in fuel costs might also have played a part in inducing some travelers to postpone or cancel their trips.

By the fourth quarter, there was a slight increase in passenger numbers as the holidays neared. Thanksgiving and Christmas, two holidays which are most significant during the United States, encouraged many to journey to be with their family and friends. Airports were crowded with travelers as holiday shoppers brimming with presents and holiday cheer embarked on their journeys. This expansion, however, was not sufficient to reach the highs of the second quarter, and the numbers remained relatively subdued.

Lastly, during the first quarter of 2024, the number of passengers stopped at 743 thousand. The winter season normally experiences a dip in travel, as bad weather in most areas of the country can be unfavourable to air travel. Snowstorms, ice storms, and freezing temperatures result in the cancellation and delay of flights, discouraging some travelers from going on trips. Moreover, the post-holiday period usually sees a dip in travel activity as people get back to their schedules. The seasons, holidays, and the travelling public's zeal all played their part in this cyclical dance, creating a dynamic pattern of passenger traffic in these two years.

The Fare - Mileage Tango

Our investigation delved deep into the intricate relationship between mileage and fares. It's as if fares and mileage are engaged in a complex tango. Typically, they move in harmony, but there are unexpected improvisations. For example, domestic flights stretching from the east to the west coast of the United States, covering approximately 2,500 miles, usually come with a higher price tag. A prime instance is the flight from Los Angeles to New York, spanning multiple time zones. Airline operators factor in the extended flight duration, increased fuel consumption, and additional human resource needs when setting prices for these long-haul flights.

However, the scatter plot reveals a more nuanced reality. There are numerous outliers, much like a dancer briefly straying from the choreographed routine. Some short-haul flights, despite their relatively short distances, astonishingly have high fares. This phenomenon can be attributed to several factors. In areas with limited airport infrastructure or monopolistic aviation operations, competition is scarce. As a result, airline operators can charge exorbitant prices, even for flights covering just a few hundred miles. Conversely, some long-distance flights offer surprisingly affordable deals. Airlines often launch promotional campaigns or engage in price wars to attract customers. For instance, during off-peak seasons, airlines may slash prices on long-distance international or cross-country flights to fill empty seats.

Moreover, the relationship is also influenced by the type of aircraft, advance booking, and class of service. A journey on a luxury business aircraft will always cost more than a regular commercial flight, regardless of the distance. Similarly, last-minute bookings generally come with a price premium, while early-bird bookings can enjoy substantial discounts. All these elements intertwine to create a sophisticated interaction between cost and distance in the ever-evolving landscape of air travel, vividly illustrated by this scatter plot.

The Price Tag on Different Journeys

As we explored deeper into the realm of route-specific fares, we uncovered a world filled with striking contrasts. Take the extremely busy Los Angeles - New York (LAX - JFK) route as an example. Spanning approximately 2,475 miles, this cross-country flight showcases the flexibility of air travel fares. With an average high fare of $462.07 and an average low fare of $344.74, the price range is significant. The peak-fare period typically coincides with peak travel seasons, such as summer vacations and holidays, when seat demand surges. Business travelers, who often need to book last-minute for emergency meetings, also contribute to the higher fares during these times.

Conversely, lower prices on the LAX - JFK route are usually observed during off-peak travel periods. Airlines, eager to fill empty seats, offer discounted prices to attract price-conscious travelers. Additionally, the intense competition among airlines on this busy route helps drive prices down. Many carriers operate between these two cities, competing aggressively for market share through pricing strategies. When we shift our focus to other routes, factors such as distance, operating costs, and market demand become more apparent.

For example, the Miami - Chicago (MIA - ORD) route, covering about 1,200 miles, has a distinct price profile. Due to the shorter distance, operating costs are lower compared to transcontinental routes. However, the fare is still subject to seasonal fluctuations. During winter, when Chicago experiences cold weather and Miami enjoys sunny skies, the demand for the Chicago-Miami route surges. Consequently, fares on this route are relatively high, catering to those seeking a respite from the cold. Routes between smaller cities with lower demand frequencies may have different fare structures. These routes often operate fewer flights, and airlines may need to set higher prices to cover their operating costs.

Even a relatively short journey between two less-frequented cities could have a higher average fare due to the lack of economies of scale. Thus, each route is like a unique path in a vast forest, with its own set of determinants shaping its cost, contributing to the complex tapestry of the domestic air travel price structure.

The Extremes of Pricing

As we delved into the highest and lowest fares of various routes, it was like catching a glimpse of the peaks and valleys of a mountainous terrain. Among all the routes, the Los Angeles - New York (LAX - JFK) route once again caught our attention. With a high fare of $442 and a low fare of $399, this flight exemplifies the intricacies of US air transportation pricing.

The highest fares on the LAX - JFK route often occur during peak travel seasons. In summer, when families plan long-awaited vacations and business conferences are in full swing, the demand for flights surges. Airlines seize this opportunity to raise fares, capitalizing on the high demand and limited seat availability. Last-minute bookings, typically made by business travelers with urgent travel needs, also contribute to the elevated fares. On the other hand, the lowest fares on this route are usually offered during low-season months or as part of promotional campaigns. Airlines offer discounted fares to boost sales and fill otherwise empty seats.

Let's consider another flight route, San Francisco - Orlando (SFO - MCO), to observe similar yet different trends. Orlando, known for its pleasant winter weather and world-famous theme parks, attracts a large number of visitors during winter. This high demand can drive the peak fare on the SFO - MCO route up to $500. However, during the off-peak summer months, when the Florida sun can be scorching, airlines reduce fares to as low as $250 to attract budget-conscious travelers.

The impact of external events on fare extremes cannot be overstated. For instance, during holidays like Thanksgiving or Christmas, the demand for all routes surges, leading to higher maximum fares. Conversely, unforeseen events such as natural disasters, public health crises, or financial crises can cause a sudden drop in demand, forcing airlines to implement rock-bottom minimum fares to stay afloat. These varying fare levels across different routes highlight the stark contrast between pricing policies and the dynamic market forces at play in the US domestic airline travel market.

The Seasonal Swing of Fares

As the quarters passed, fares exhibited a dynamic pattern, almost as if they had a life of their own. In the second quarter of 2023, with the arrival of summer, fares reached a record average of $257.0. This upward trend was likely driven by the summer break season, as families sought to escape the ordinary and embark on vacations. Popular resort towns along the California coast and cosmopolitan cities in the Northeast witnessed a surge in demand. Airlines, capitalizing on this increased demand, adjusted their pricing models, leading to a rise in average fares.

However, as the third quarter arrived and the summer heat began to wane, fares dropped to an average of $241.3. With the peak vacation period over and schools back in session, air travel demand declined. Airlines responded by offering more competitive pricing to maintain passenger traffic. The changing weather also played a role, as destinations that were popular in summer became less appealing with the approach of autumn.

By the fourth quarter, fares made a remarkable comeback, rising to an average of $253.2. The holiday season, particularly Thanksgiving and Christmas, was the primary catalyst for this resurgence. These holidays are deeply ingrained in American culture, prompting millions of people to travel across the country to be with their families. The increased demand for air travel, coupled with limited seat availability due to high passenger volumes, allowed airlines to raise prices once again.

This ebb and flow of fares, as depicted in the line chart, reveals the intricate relationship between travel demand, seasonality, and airline pricing policies. This information is invaluable to airlines aiming to maximize revenue, travel agents seeking to offer the best fares to their customers, and consumers planning their trips. Our exploration of the seasonal patterns of fares contributes to a more comprehensive understanding of the complex landscape of US domestic air travel, illustrating how market forces continuously shape the cost of flights.
